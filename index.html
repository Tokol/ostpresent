Geometric Foundation

Why Circles Matter

Constant radius. A circle has a constant radius from its center, enabling uniform rotation and even stress distribution.

Maximum area. For a given boundary, a circle encloses the maximum area, making it an efficient and stable design choice.

Circular patterns appear across scales, from microscopic structures to large astronomical systems, though rarely as perfect circles.

Roundness Error

Perfect Circle vs Reality

Perfect circle. A perfect circle is a mathematical ideal with a constant radius at every point.

Manufacturing reality. In real-world manufacturing, no object is perfectly circular. Deviations arise from machining, material behavior, and process variation.

This deviation is what we define and measure as roundness error.

To evaluate this, standards such as ISO 12181 define methods to compute roundness.

Reference Circle Method

LSC — Least Squares Circle (Best Fit)

Core idea. LSC finds a circle that best fits all measured points by balancing deviations across the entire shape.

Analogy. Like kids playing with a hula hoop, LSC adjusts the hoop so the overall distance to all points is minimized, not pulled by extremes.

No single point dominates the shape. The circle represents a balanced fit.

Least Squares Method

How LSC is Computed

For every point, we measure how far it is from the circle. Then we square those differences and sum them. The best circle is the one where this total error is smallest.

E = ∑ni=1(di − r)2

What the terms mean

  • dᵢ → distance from point to center
  • r → radius of the circle
  • dᵢ − r → deviation from the circle
  • Squaring ensures all deviations contribute positively

Reference Circle Method

MCC — Minimum Circumscribed Circle (Outer Constraint)

Core idea. MCC finds the smallest circle that contains all measured points.

Analogy. Like stretching a rubber band around a group of nails so it tightly wraps the outermost ones.

The circle is controlled by the outermost points, not the average.

Minimum Circumscribed Circle

How MCC is Computed

We measure distances from all points, but only the largest one matters. That point decides how big the circle must be.

r = maxi(di)

What the terms mean

  • dᵢ → distance from point to center
  • r → radius of enclosing circle
  • The farthest point defines the circle

Reference Circle Method

MIC — Maximum Inscribed Circle (Inner Constraint)

Core idea. MIC finds the largest possible circle that fits completely inside all measured points.

Analogy. Like placing the largest possible coin inside an irregular hole without crossing its boundary.

The circle is controlled by the innermost points, not the outer shape.

Maximum Inscribed Circle

How MIC is Computed

We measure all distances, but the smallest one limits the circle. That point decides how big the circle can be

r = mini(di)

What the terms mean

  • dᵢ → distance from point to center
  • r → radius of inscribed circle
  • The nearest point limits the size

Reference Circle Method

MZC — Minimum Zone Circle (True Roundness Error)

Core idea. MZC finds two concentric circles such that all measured points lie between them, and the gap between the circles is as small as possible.

Analogy. Like placing points inside a thin ring and adjusting the ring until it becomes as tight as possible around all points.

Roundness is defined by the smallest possible radial zone that contains all points.

Minimum Zone Circle

How Roundness Error is Computed (MZC)

We look for two circles with the same center. The difference between their radii is the roundness error.

E = rmax − rmin

What the terms mean

  • rmax → radius of outer circle (farthest point)
  • rmin → radius of inner circle (nearest point)
  • E → roundness error (zone thickness)

Practical Constraints

System Limitations and Practical Constraints

Fixture and Method Validation

Fixture improves repeatability

Pipe centering and angle can still vary

LSC/MCC/MIC threshold not yet defined

Impact: final acceptance needs validated rules

Edge Detection Sensitivity

Lighting and reflections affect edges

Noise can break contour continuity

Impact: unstable fitting and roundness error

2D Geometric Limitation

Assumes planar circular cross-section

Tilt and 3D deformation are not fully captured

Impact: incomplete shape representation

The fixture improves repeatability, but inspection confidence still depends on alignment, image quality, and agreed OSTP validation thresholds.
